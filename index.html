Sub-Visible Particle Morphology Clustering

Unsupervised morphology-aware analysis of sub-visible particles in biologics.

Eugene Kim1 and Jaeyoung Lee1
1Ghent University Global Campus

The Problem with Today’s SVP Analytics

Sub-visible particles (SVPs) are proteinaceous aggregates and other particulates in the micrometre range that are treated as critical quality attributes in therapeutic biologics. They are monitored because their presence and properties can influence immunogenicity, product stability, and clinical performance.

However, in routine practice, SVP analysis is still dominated by particle counting and sizing methods that provide overall concentrations, size distributions, and a few simple shape descriptors. This means that the morphological and compositional information contained in flow imaging microscopy (FIM) data remains largely unused, limiting our ability to fully characterize SVP populations.

Clustering Strategy

Overview

We clustered several thousand images of sub-visible particles collected from ten commercially available pharmaceutical products. The goal was to explore whether particles could be grouped based solely on visual similarity, without prior labeling or assumptions about particle type.

Unsupervised Image-Based Grouping

An unsupervised clustering algorithm was applied to image-derived features, grouping particles according to similarities in their visual appearance. This approach allows patterns to emerge directly from the data, rather than being imposed by predefined categories.

Morphological Consistency Within Clusters

Within each resulting cluster, particles exhibited broadly consistent morphological characteristics. These similarities suggest that the clustering captured shared visual features present across the dataset, such as shape, texture, or structural complexity.

High-Level View of Particle Diversity

Organization of thousands of images into a limited number of visually coherent groups provides a high-level overview of particle diversity across products. This structure serves as a foundation for further qualitative and quantitative analysis.

Future Works

Visual Separation Between Particle Types

The visual separation between clusters arises from grouping particles based on shared image-derived features. Particles within a cluster consistently exhibited similar characteristics, such as shape and texture, while clear differences were observed between clusters, indicating reproducible morphological patterns rather than arbitrary grouping.

Uneven Cluster Populations

Cluster sizes varied substantially, with some morphologies occurring far more frequently than others. This uneven distribution likely reflects the natural variability of particle populations present in the dataset.

Ambiguous and Unclustered Particles

Some particles could not be confidently assigned to a cluster due to mixed or intermediate visual features. These unclustered images likely represent transitional morphologies or overlapping particle types, highlighting the continuous nature of particle appearance.

Practical Value of Morphology-Based Clustering

Overall, the clustering framework provides a practical way to reduce the complexity of large image datasets while preserving meaningful morphological variation. It enables efficient exploration of dominant particle types while retaining flexibility for ambiguous cases.
